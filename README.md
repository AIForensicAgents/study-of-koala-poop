# study-of-koala-poop

A field-study, educational, and reference repository documenting koala scat (scat, droppings, feces) for biological, ecological, and citizen-science purposes — serious science, with a wink.

---

## Overview

This repository collects notes, photographs, and practical guidance for identifying and interpreting koala feces in the field. It is intended for educators, students, wildlife biologists, and community scientists who want to understand how scat can inform us about koala diet, health, and habitat use.

Contents:
- Field-safe morphology notes and interpretation tips
- Habitat and dietary clues that scat can reveal
- Practical ethics and safety guidance for non-invasive study
- A curated image gallery for training and reference
- Suggested directions for future research and community projects

This is an educational resource — not a substitute for a formal wildlife survey protocol or species-specific permitting.

---

## Why it matters

Koala scat is more than “nature’s breadcrumbs.” When studied responsibly, it can:
- Reveal diet composition and preferences (which eucalypt species are being eaten)
- Indicate health and nutritional status (consistency and color changes)
- Inform microbial and parasite research (gut microbiome, pathogenic presence)
- Support non-invasive population monitoring and habitat use inference
- Enable community science participation without needing to capture animals

Understanding scat helps protect koalas by offering low-impact ways to monitor populations and habitat quality.

## Morphology notes

Use these field-friendly markers to describe and record specimens. Always photograph in situ before any handling.

Typical features
- General form: ranges from soft, pasty "pap" (especially from joeys or sick animals) to firm, small pellets.
- Size & shape: often pellet-like or clumped; pellet size can vary with age and diet — juveniles produce smaller/softer pieces.
- Color: varies with diet and hydration — fresh droppings tend toward greenish-brown; older samples darken.
- Texture & consistency: fresh scat for healthy adults is firm but not rock-hard; very soft or watery feces can indicate illness or dietary shift.
- Odor: eucalyptus chemical compounds (eucalyptol) may be present; strikingly foul or unusual odors warrant professional attention.
- Contents: visible leaf fragments, fibrous matter, twig bits; presence/absence of undigested plant parts can suggest digestive efficiency or diet type.

Recording tips
- Take scale photos (ruler or coin) and note distance to nearest koala sighting if applicable.
- Log GPS, time/date, tree species nearby, and weather conditions.
- Note whether you see “pap”: soft, nutrient-rich feces produced by adult females for joeys — an important reproductive behavior to record.

## Habitat / diet clues

Scat can be a powerful indicator of the local ecology.

What to look for
- Eucalypt-specific residues: different eucalyptus species impart subtle dietary signatures; note the tree species in the immediate area.
- Foraging patterns: concentration of droppings beneath commonly used feeding trees suggests preferred browse species.
- Seasonal shifts: changes in pellet composition, quantity, or location across seasons can reflect shifts in dietary breadth or resource availability.
- Spatial clues: scat distribution along travel routes or around resting trees can help map habitat use without direct observation.

Analytical directions
- Visual diet inference: coarse assessment from leaf fragments and fiber.
- Laboratory analyses: pollen analysis, stable isotope work, or DNA metabarcoding for precise plant-species identification (requires permits and lab collaboration).

## Ethics & Safety

This is non-invasive field research — prioritize animal welfare, biosecurity, and personal safety.

Do
- Observe from a distance and do not disturb resting or feeding koalas.
- Photograph scat in place before handling.
- Use disposable gloves when collecting samples; change between sites.
- Follow local laws and obtain permits for sample collection or export.
- Decontaminate tools and boots between sites to limit disease spread and invasive species transfer.
- Label samples carefully with metadata (date, time, GPS, collector, notes).

Don't
- Chase or harass koalas to get samples or photos.
- Taste or ingest any sample (obvious, but necessary).
- Assume samples are safe — zoonotic pathogens and parasites can be present.
- Share raw location data of sensitive populations publicly if it may increase poaching or disturbance risk; follow data-sharing best practices (e.g., coarse-grain sensitive coordinates).

First-aid & contamination
- If handling leads to a puncture or exposure, clean the wound thoroughly and seek medical advice.
- Follow institutional biosafety protocols for transport and analysis of biological material.

## Future work

Planned and suggested extensions to the project:
- Build a labeled, open dataset of scat photos for training computer-vision models to assist rapid field ID.
- Collaborate with labs for DNA metabarcoding to quantify diet composition from fecal samples.
- Microbiome studies focused on gut flora differences across populations and health statuses.
- Citizen-science app or form for crowdsourced scat sightings, with built-in safety/ethics prompts and data validation.
- Protocol templates for standardized sample collection (permits, cold-chain, metadata).
- Educational modules for schools: “Koala Ecology 101” hands-on activities using non-invasive observation and photo-identification.
